When you drop a typical drinking‚Äëglass onto a hard surface (like tile, hardwood or concrete), the most likely outcome is that it **breaks into multiple shards**.

### Why it breaks

1. **Brittle material** ‚Äì Glass is a glassy, amorphous solid. It withstands compression well but offers very little plastic deformation. When a sudden impact occurs, the high localized stresses exceed the material strength, and cracks nucleate and propagate rapidly.

2. **Energy transfer** ‚Äì The gravitational potential energy before the drop is converted into kinetic energy as the glass falls, then into elastic deformation and finally into fracture work. A hard floor offers little resistance, so most energy goes into creating new surfaces (shards) rather than absorbing it elastically.

3. **Surface defects** ‚Äì Even a small flaw, scratch or internal void can act as a stress concentrator and become the initiation point for a crack once the impact stress exceeds the glass‚Äôs tensile strength.

### What it can look like

- **Sharp, broken edges** ‚Äì Often a pattern of radial fractures spreading out from the impact point.
- **Splat of smaller pieces** ‚Äì When the glass shatters, small fragments scatter, sometimes landing a few feet away.
- **Shards stuck to the floor** ‚Äì Glass often doesn‚Äôt bounce; high‚Äëspeed impacts send the pieces in various directions but most stay on the floor.

### Counter‚Äëexamples

- **Tempered glass** ‚Äì Designed to crack into small, blunt pieces that are less dangerous. Even tempered glass usually breaks when dropped hard enough, but fragments are safer.
- **Drop on a soft surface** ‚Äì Carpet, rubber, or a plush cushion can absorb enough energy to reduce the peak stress, sometimes preventing breakage.
