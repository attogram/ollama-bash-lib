## Explain the theory of relativity in very simple terms.

**The Theory of Relativity in a nutshell**

1. **Special Relativity (1915)**  
   - *Speed of light is constant*: No matter how fast you‚Äôre moving, light always travels at the same speed (‚âà‚ÄØ300‚ÄØ000‚ÄØkm/s).  
   - *Time and space are linked*: If you‚Äôre moving close to that speed, time for you runs slower and distances appear shorter compared to a stationary observer.  
   - *Mass‚Äìenergy equivalence*: `E = mc¬≤`. A small amount of mass can be turned into a huge amount of energy‚Äîwhat we see in the Sun and in nuclear power.

2. **General Relativity (1915)**  
   - *Gravity is not a ‚Äúpush‚Äù or ‚Äúpull‚Äù*: Massive objects (like the Sun) bend the fabric of space‚Äëtime around them.  
   - *Objects follow the bent paths*: Just as a marble rolls along a curved track, planets orbit the Sun because the space‚Äëtime around the Sun is curved.  
   - *Light follows the curvature too*: That‚Äôs why starlight bends when it passes near the Sun (gravitational lensing) and why the whole Solar System is slightly warped.

**Simple Picture**

- Imagine a stretched rubber sheet. Put a heavy ball on it ‚Üí the sheet dips.  
- Roll a smaller ball across: it curves toward the heavy ball, not because the heavy ball is ‚Äúattracting‚Äù it, but because the sheet is curved.  
- That‚Äôs how the universe works: objects move along the curvatures made by mass, and the speed of light stays the same no matter how you travel.

*Bottom line*: Relativity tells us that space and time are one connected thing (space‚Äëtime), that they can stretch and bend, and that the laws of physics stay the same for everyone, no matter how fast they‚Äôre moving.

## If all cats are animals and some animals are playful, can we say some cats are playful?

‚ÄúNo, we cannot conclude that some cats are playful.‚Äù

**Why?**

1. **First premise**  
   *All cats are animals.*  
   \[
   \forall x\,(\text{Cat}(x) \rightarrow \text{Animal}(x))
   \]

2. **Second premise**  
   *Some animals are playful.*  
   \[
   \exists x\,(\text{Animal}(x) \land \text{Playful}(x))
   \]

These two statements together do **not** force the existence of an animal that is **both** a cat and playful. In other words, the fact that there is at least one playful animal does not tell us anything about whether that animal is a cat.

### A quick Venn‚Äëdiagram picture

```
          Animals
        _____________
       |             |
       |   Cats      |
       |_____________|

   Playful animals overlap with ‚ÄúAnimals‚Äù, but we cannot see the
   overlap with ‚ÄúCats‚Äù.
```

### Counterexample

Suppose:

- All cats are indeed animals.  
- Only the dogs in the world are playful.  

Then the premises are true:

- All cats are animals ‚úîÔ∏è  
- Some animals (the dogs) are playful ‚úîÔ∏è  

But *no cat* is playful, so the conclusion ‚Äúsome cats are playful‚Äù would be false.

### Logical form

From  
\[
\forall x(\text{Cat}(x) \to \text{Animal}(x)) \quad\text{and}\quad \exists x(\text{Animal}(x) \land \text{Playful}(x)),
\]
we cannot derive  
\[
\exists x(\text{Cat}(x) \land \text{Playful}(x)).
\]

Only an additional bridge statement such as ‚Äúall playful animals are cats‚Äù or ‚Äúsome cats are playful‚Äù would allow that inference.

---

**Bottom line:** The premises leave the possibility open that the playful animals might be completely separate from cats. Therefore we cannot assert that some cats are playful based solely on the given information.

## Write a Bash function that returns the sum of a list of numbers.

Below is a small Bash helper you can drop into any script (or into your `~/.bashrc` / `~/.bash_profile` so it‚Äôs always available).  
It sums the numbers passed as arguments and prints the sum to **stdout**.

```bash
# --------------------------------------------------------
# sum() ‚Äì return the sum of all numbers provided on the command line
# --------------------------------------------------------
sum() {
    local total=0          # accumulator

    # Iterate over every positional parameter ($1, $2, ‚Ä¶)
    for n in "$@"; do
        # ‚Äú(( ‚Ä¶ ))‚Äù does integer arithmetic in Bash.
        # It gracefully handles +, -, *, /, and also handles
        # leading/trailing spaces, leading zeros, etc.
        (( total += n ))
    done

    # Print the result so the caller can capture it with
    #   result=$(sum 1 2 3)   (or `echo "$(sum ...)"` if you prefer)
    printf "%d\n" "$total"
}
```

### How to use it

```bash
# Simple integer sum
result=$(sum 4 12 -3 9)
echo "Total: $result"         # ‚Üí Total: 22

# With a pipe
echo "5 10 15" | tr ' ' '\n' | xargs sum
# ‚Üí 30

# Passing an array
nums=(1 2 3 4 5)
echo "Sum of array: $(sum "${nums[@]}")"
```

|---------|----------------|

| **Handles negative numbers** | Negatives are added/subtracted correctly. |
| **Large integers up to 2¬≥¬π‚àí1** | Bash arithmetic keeps values in the range of your architecture‚Äôs native integer type. For larger numbers, see the ‚ÄúDecimal & Big‚ÄëInt‚Äù block below. |
| **Easy to test/capture** | The function prints the result, so you can capture it into a variable or use it directly in a pipeline. |

## Optional: Decimals & arbitrary‚Äësize integers

The builtin arithmetics only support integers. If you need decimal numbers or integers bigger than your shell‚Äôs native range, you‚Äôll need an external tool such as `bc`.

```bash

    local total="0"
    for n in "$@"; do
        # Use `bc` for decimal addition; -l loads the math library.
        total=$(echo "$total + $n" | bc -l)
    done
    echo "$total"
}
```

Usage:

```bash

# ‚Üí 6.875
```

## If you drop a glass on the floor, what is most likely to happen?

If you drop a typical glass (like a drink cup or a kitchenware piece) on a hard floor‚Äîtile, hardwood, concrete, etc.‚Äîthe most common outcome is that it breaks or shatters.  

Why?  
- The glass is a brittle material. When it hits the floor, the impact creates a rapid spike in stress that usually exceeds the material‚Äôs tensile strength.  
- The resulting crack quickly propagates, sometimes through the whole piece (shattering), sometimes leaving a single crack or a few shards.

So the usual scenario is: the glass falls, strikes the floor, and splits into pieces with sharp fragments that can be dangerous.  

Only special glasses (very thick, tempered, or deliberately designed for impact resistance) or very low drop heights might let a glass survive without breaking. But in everyday life, a dropped glass on a hard floor will most likely break.

## A rectangle has a length of 10 meters and a width of 4 meters. What is its area?

The area of a rectangle is calculated by multiplying its length by its width.

\[
\text{Area} = \text{length} \times \text{width} = 10\,\text{m} \times 4\,\text{m} = 40\,\text{m}^2
\]

**The area is 40‚ÄØsquare‚ÄØmeters.**
